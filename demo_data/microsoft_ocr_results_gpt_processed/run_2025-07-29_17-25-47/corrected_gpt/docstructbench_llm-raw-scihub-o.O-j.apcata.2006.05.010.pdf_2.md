Catalysts employed for hydrocracking of F-T waxes are similar to those conventionally used for hydrocracking of petroleum-derived feeds, that is, they are of bifunctional nature, comprising a metal component dispersed on an acidic porous support [4]. Since the F-T products are almost free of sulfur, the preferred metal phase is a noble metal, such as Pt and Pd, while amorphous oxides such as SiO2-Al2O3 [5-8], and zeolites (Y, beta, mordenite) [9-14] are commonly used as acidic carriers. When hydroisomerization instead of hydrocracking is the main target of the long-chain $n$-alkane hydroconversion step, as it occurs in isodewaxing processes, catalysts based on unidirectional 10-member ring zeolites (ZSM-22, ZSM-23, ZSM-35, ZSM-48) [15-18] or silicoaluminophosphate molecular sieves (SAPO-11, SAPO-31, SAPO-41) [19-21] as acidic supports have been proposed. A review dealing with suitable catalysts for the hydroisomerization of long-chain $n$-paraffins has been recently published by Deldari [22].

On a well-balanced catalyst, the hydrocracking activity and selectivity mainly depends on the acid properties of the support. Thus, catalysts based on amorphous silica-alumina having moderate Bronsted acidity are more selective towards middle distillates but are less active than those based on zeolites [4,23-25]. In the case of zeolitic catalysts, improved selectivity towards middle distillates can be attained by reducing the density of Bronsted acid sites, that is, by increasing the Si/Al ratio [26,27], or by decreasing their acid strength through partial or total isomorphous substitution of framework Al3+ by other trivalent cations such as $\mathrm{B}^{3+}$ [28]. However, the gain in selectivity obtained after reducing the zeolite acidity is always accompanied by a decrease in the catalyst activity, thus requiring higher reaction temperatures to achieve a given degree of conversion.

Therefore, there is still great interest in developing new catalysts for the hydrocracking-hydroisomerization of high-boiling point $n$-paraffins (waxes), such as those produced in the F-T process, which exhibit high activity at reaction temperatures as low as possible while keeping a good selectivity towards the desired hydrocracked fraction, i.e. middle distillates. In this sense, it has been recently reported that a Pt/WOx-ZrO2 catalyst (0.5 wt.% Pt, 6.5 wt.% W) prepared by impregnation and calcined at 700 °C displays high activity and good selectivity towards branched isomers in the diesel and lube-base oil range during the hydrocracking of Fischer-Tropsch waxes [29]. Unfortunately, while the behaviour of Pt/WOx-ZrO2 catalysts for the hydroisomerization of short-chain $n$-alkanes ($C_{4}-C_{7}$) is well documented in the literature [30-41], the amount of work dealing with heavier $n$-alkanes that would be more representative of the waxy Fischer-Tropsch products is very scarce [29,42-45].

The catalytic properties of Pt/WOx-ZrO2 materials are largely influenced by the method of preparation, chemical composition, and activation conditions. Then, it is not surprising that different optimum compositions (W loading) are found in the literature depending on the method of preparation and calcination temperature [31,46-49]. These discrepancies can be partly reconciled by considering the tungsten surface density (W atoms/nm$^2$), which can be modified by varying the W loading and/or calcination temperature, instead of the W loading. When this parameter is considered, a maximum in the isomerization activity is generally found at W surface densities close or slightly exceeding the monolayer coverage of zirconia by tungstate, provided the material has been previously calcined at a sufficiently high temperature, typically in the range of 700-900 °C [32,50-52]. Nevertheless, Santiesteban et al. [48] reported that a sample prepared by simultaneous coprecipitation of tungsten and hydrous zirconia displayed twice the number of strong Brönsted acid sites and catalytic activity for $n$-pentane isomerization than a catalyst prepared by impregnation of W over a previously refluxed hydrous zirconia, despite both materials presented similar surface area and W loading, and thus similar W surface density.

Taking into account the above discussion, in this work we address the influence of the tungsten coverage on the catalytic behaviour of Pt/WOx-ZrO2 catalysts for the hydroconversion of $n$-hexadecane, taken as representative molecule of the long-chain $n$-alkanes produced in F-T processes. The $\mathrm{WO}_x-\mathrm{ZrO}_2$ samples used here have been prepared by coprecipitation in the presence of polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) leading to samples with enhanced specific surface areas as compared to conventional coprecipitation and impregnation methods [53]. Interestingly, we have found that the tungsten coverage in $\mathrm{PtWO}_x-\mathrm{ZrO}_2$ catalysts has a strong influence on the product selectivity when compared at constant conversion. To our best knowledge, this aspect has never been discussed in previous works. Finally, the catalytic performance of $\mathrm{Pt/WO}_x-\mathrm{ZrO}_2$ has been compared with that of bifunctional catalysts based on a commercial amorphous silica-alumina (ASA) and a low unit cell size USY zeolite ($a_0 = 2.433 \mathrm{nm}$).

# 2. Experimental

## 2.1. Preparation of catalysts

A series of tungsten-doped zirconia samples (abbreviated as WZ) with tungsten loading in the range of 6-22 wt.% W have been prepared by coprecipitation in the presence of polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) following the procedure described in Ref. [53] with some modifications. Chiefly, 300 cm$^3$ of a 0.53 M zirconyl chloride (Aldrich, 98% ZrOCl2.8H2O) and ammonium metatungstate $\left( \left( \mathrm{NH}_4 \right)_6 \mathrm{H}_2 \mathrm{W}_{12} \mathrm{O}_{40} \right.$, Fluka, ≥85% WO3) aqueous solutions were heated at 100 °C in an oil bath and kept at this temperature for 1 h. Besides, 300 cm$^3$ of a polyvinyl alcohol $\mathrm{(PVA, MW:} 7 \times 10^4 - 9 \times 10^4$, Sigma) aqueous solution (100 g/L) were heated at 100 °C in an oil bath for 1 h. Then, the two solutions were mixed and kept at 100 °C for an additional hour. After that, the mixed solution was cooled until 70 °C, slowly added to a $\mathrm{NH}_4\mathrm{OH}$ (2 M)/$\mathrm{NH}_4\mathrm{Cl}$ (2 M) buffer solution to keep the pH constant at 10, and kept for one additional hour in static. The formed transparent gel filaments were extensively washed with distilled water until the absence of chlorides and dried at 100 °C overnight and at 200 °C for an additional 12 h period.
